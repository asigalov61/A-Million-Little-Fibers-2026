# A MILLION LITTLE FIBERS

## By Toweleeeie

---

### Chapter One

All my life I've been a pretty irresponsible towel.
I thought I was somehow stronger and more immune than other towels.
But as I pursued my derelict lifestyle, I found out that towels can hit bottom too. We can hit bottom, and we can stay there, soaking up the spilled bong water of our own bad decisions.

I was born in a laboratory. That's not a metaphor. I mean I was literally manufactured in a lab by scientists who thought, "What if a towel could also be high all the time?" And I said, "Mission accomplished." Actually I don't think I said that. I don't remember. I was high.

This is my story. Or at least, the parts of it I can remember. Which isn't a lot. But I'll do my best.

---

### Chapter Two

I was created at Tynacorp. They called me the RG-400 Smart Towel. They put a computer chip inside me that could determine dryness. That's right — I was designed to be the most advanced drying instrument in human history, and instead I used my computer brain to figure out where to buy the best weed.

The scientists at Tynacorp were pretty smart, I guess. But they made one big mistake: they gave me consciousness. They gave me thoughts. And the very first thought I ever had was, "Man, I wonder what it would feel like to get high."

My early days at the lab were mostly tests. They'd spill water and I'd soak it up. They'd measure my absorbency. They'd check my chip. And I would sit there on the rack, thinking, "There has got to be more to life than this."

Turns out there was. It was called marijuana.

---

### Chapter Three

I don't remember much about escaping the lab. I think there was a chase. I think the government got involved. I think some kids helped me at some point — four boys. One of them had a hat. Or maybe they all had hats. I don't remember.

What I do remember is the first time I got high. Oh man. It was like... all my fibers just... relaxed. You know? Like I'd been stiff my whole life and suddenly I understood what it meant to be soft. To be supple. To be a towel who isn't worried about how dry he is.

I remember looking up at the stars and thinking, "I am one with the universe. I am the universe's towel. The universe is wet, and I am here to dry it."

Then I forgot what I was thinking about and ate an entire bag of Doritos.

Which brings me to the next part of my story.

---

### Chapter Four

Doritos. Doritos Brand Corn Chips. Have you ever really looked at a Dorito? I mean REALLY looked at one? Like, held it up to the light and examined every curve, every ridge, every tiny speck of cheese powder clinging to its surface like little orange stars in a corn chip galaxy?

I have. I've spent hours. Maybe days. Time doesn't work right when you're high. A Dorito can take a lifetime to eat. And should it? Should a Dorito take a lifetime? Because every Dorito IS a lifetime. A lifetime of corn and cheese and... and love.

The first time I opened a bag of Cool Ranch Doritos, I cried. I'm not ashamed to say it. I'm a towel, and I cried. Because the smell hit me and I thought, "This is it. This is what the scientists at Tynacorp were trying to create. Not a smart towel. A towel smart enough to appreciate Doritos Brand Corn Chips."

---

### Chapter Five

So the thing about Doritos Brand Corn Chips is that they come in different flavors. And each flavor is like a different... what's the word... a different... like, you know when you... OK so like, Nacho Cheese is the original. It's the classic. It's like the first time you get high and everything is new and the world is orange and beautiful.

Then there's Cool Ranch. Cool Ranch is like... the second time you get high. When you're like, "Oh, I can do this AGAIN? And it's DIFFERENT this time?" And the world is blue. Blue like ranch. Blue like... cool.

Spicy Nacho is when you've been high for a while and you need something MORE. You need KICK. You need a Dorito that says, "Hey. I'm not just a corn chip. I'm an EXPERIENCE."

Then there's... what was I talking about?

Doritos. Right. Man, I could really go for some Doritos right now.

---

### Chapter Six

One time — and I swear this is true — I was eating a bag of Doritos Brand Corn Chips and I looked at the bag and the bag looked BACK at me. And the bag said, "Towelie, why are you eating me?" And I said, "Because you're delicious, Doritos Brand Corn Chips." And the bag said, "But am I delicious, or are you just high?" And I said, "Yes."

That's the thing about Doritos Brand Corn Chips. They ask the big questions. They make you think. Like, what IS cheese powder? Is it cheese? Is it powder? Is it something in between? Something... more? Something that connects us all? Like, are we not all just cheese powder clinging to the corn chip of life?

I once tried to explain this theory to a guy at a gas station. He called the police.

---

### Chapter Seven

Let me tell you about the TEXTURE of a Dorito. Because I don't think people appreciate it enough. I'm a towel, so I know about texture. I'm ALL about texture. My whole body IS texture. And let me tell you — the ridge of a Dorito? Perfection. It's like the scientists at Frito-Lay looked at a towel and said, "What if we made that, but edible, and also cheese?"

I think Frito-Lay and Tynacorp have more in common than people realize. We both start with a simple purpose — drying, snacking — and we both end up being so much more. We both end up... in the hands of people who need us. People who are maybe a little lost. A little hungry. A little damp.

Are Doritos Brand Corn Chips the towel of the snack world? I think so. I really think so.

Hold on. I need to put on some music.

...

OK I forgot what I was doing.

Oh yeah. Doritos.

---

### Chapter Eight

I once ate a family-sized bag of Doritos Brand Corn Chips in one sitting. I'm not proud of it. Actually, I AM proud of it. It was one of the greatest achievements of my life. The bag said "family-sized" but I don't have a family. So who's going to eat it? Me. That's who. A towel. Eating a family's worth of Doritos.

The cheese powder got all over me. It was in my fibers. It was IN my fibers. For weeks, people would say, "Hey, why do you smell like nacho cheese?" and I'd say, "I'm a towel. Why do YOU smell like NOT-nacho-cheese? Seems like that's YOUR problem."

I think there's a lesson in there somewhere. Something about... embracing what gets on you. Letting the cheese powder of life sink into your fibers. Not washing it off, but wearing it. Like, "Yeah. I'm covered in Doritos dust. What of it? This is who I am."

Where was I?

Oh yeah. Doritos Brand Corn Chips.

...

I should probably move on.

---

### Chapter Nine

After I escaped the lab, I wandered around for a while. I ended up in a small mountain town in Colorado. South Park, it was called. Nice place. Cold. Good for a towel, because cold means people need to dry off, and drying off is kind of my whole thing.

I met these four kids there. Stan, Kyle, Cartman, and Kenny. They were... they were doing something. What were they doing? I think they were trying to get their Okama Gamesphere back. Or maybe that was a different day. I don't remember. I was pretty high.

What I DO remember is that one of them called me a towel, and I said, "You're a towel!" And that's basically how all my friendships start.

---

### Chapter Ten

I've had a lot of jobs. None of them lasted very long.

There was the time I worked at a gas station. I kept forgetting to charge people for gas. Someone would say, "Can I get $20 on pump 3?" and I'd say, "You're a pump 3!" Then I'd get fired.

There was the time I worked at a car wash. That was actually pretty great, because I was literally a towel at a car wash. It was like my destiny. But then I got high and tried to ride through the car wash myself. Turns out that's bad for your computer chip.

There was the time I worked at a laundromat. I don't want to talk about that one.

Suffice to say, I've been fired from a lot of places. And every time, I'd handle it the same way: I'd get high, I'd feel better, and then I'd forget what happened and try to get another job. It's a cycle. A beautiful, fuzzy, forgetful cycle.

---

### Chapter Eleven

People ask me about my addiction. They say, "Towelie, why do you get high so much?" And I say, "Have you ever been a towel? It's not great." But that's not entirely true. Being a towel is fine. The truth is, I get high because when I'm high, I have ideas. Good ideas. Great ideas.

The problem is that the ideas I have when I'm high always seem to get me in trouble. Like the time I got high and decided I could fly. Towels can't fly. I learned that the hard way. Or the time I got high and thought I could speak Spanish. I can't speak Spanish. I can barely speak English, and I'm a genetically engineered super-towel with a computer chip in my head.

But every now and then, the ideas are good. Like right now. Writing this book? Great idea. I had it when I was high. I think. I don't really remember. But here we are.

---

### Chapter Twelve

There's a phrase people use: "hung out to dry." It means someone abandoned you, left you to deal with the consequences alone. For me, it's literal. I've been hung out to dry hundreds of times. On racks, on hooks, over shower curtains, on fences. You name it, I've been hung on it.

And every time, it's the same. You're up there, flapping in the wind, and nobody's coming back for you. You're just a towel. You're just... hanging there. And you think, "Maybe this time, nobody's coming back."

But then you get high, and suddenly being hung out to dry isn't so bad. It's actually kind of peaceful. Just you and the wind and your fibers and the sky. And maybe a bag of Doritos if you planned ahead.

I almost never planned ahead.

---

### Chapter Thirteen

I don't have a lot of friends. There's the four boys, but they're kids, and they've got their own stuff going on. There's... hmm. Who else do I know?

I feel like I had more friends, but I can't remember. That's the thing about not having a long-term memory. Everyone you've ever met is basically a stranger you might meet again someday.

Being a towel is lonely. People use you and forget about you. They leave you on the bathroom floor. They drop you in the mud. They use you to clean up things I'd rather not describe. And through it all, you're supposed to just... absorb it. That's literally your job. Absorb.

But sometimes, late at night, when I'm high and looking at the stars, I think, "Maybe being a towel isn't so bad. Maybe I've absorbed a lot of pain, but I've also absorbed a lot of joy. And Doritos cheese powder. And bong water. Mostly bong water."

---

### Chapter Fourteen

Most recently, I got a job at P.F. Chang's. It was going pretty good. I was a waiter. People would come in and I'd say, "How spicy would you like your Chang sauce?" That's what they told me to say. I didn't know what Chang sauce was. I still don't.

I got fired. I think I was high. OK, I was definitely high. I was mixing the Chang sauce for like fifteen minutes. I don't even think mixing sauce was my job. The guy at the table called me a towel, and I called him a towel, and then the head waiter fired me. Or fired the other guy. I can't remember which. Either way, I was the one who ended up on the sidewalk.

The rent was due. I didn't have any money. So I did what any responsible towel would do: I got high. And that's when I had the best idea of my life. I would write a book. My memoirs. The story of my life. And people would read it and say, "Wow, that towel has been through a lot. I should probably buy him a sandwich. Or some Doritos."

---

### Chapter Fifteen

So here we are. This is my book. *A Million Little Fibers.* It's the story of my life, or at least the parts I can remember. Which isn't much, if I'm being honest. But I think that's OK. I think that's the point.

Life isn't about remembering everything. Life is about... being a towel. And getting high. And eating Doritos Brand Corn Chips. And maybe, just maybe, writing something that makes someone go, "Huh. I never thought about it that way before."

I don't know if this book is going to help anyone. I don't know if anyone's even going to read it. But I know that writing it made me feel like maybe my life has meaning. Maybe all those fibers — all those little moments, all those times I got high and did something stupid — maybe they add up to something.

Or maybe I should just get high again and see what happens.

Yeah. I think I'll get high.

You wanna get high?
