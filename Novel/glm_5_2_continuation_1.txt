# A MILLION LITTLE FIBERS

## By

## Toweleeeie

---

# Chapter One

All my life I've been a pretty irresponsible towel.
I thought I was somehow stronger and more immune than other towels.
But as I pursued my derelict

lifestyle, I discovered something about myself. I discovered that I have a million little fibers, and every single one of them has a story to tell.

Most of those stories involve getting high. But some of them involve other things. Like Doritos. And getting high while eating Doritos.

My name is Towelie. I'm a towel. I was engineered in a laboratory with a computer chip that determines dryness. I can talk, I can think, and I can get high. Those first two things make me special. That third thing makes me write books.

This is the true story of my life. Every word of it is real. I think. I don't really remember.

---

# Chapter Two

I was born in a lab. Tynacorp, they called it. A company that made smart towels for the government. Why the government needed talking towels, I don't know. Maybe the president got tired of regular towels that just sat there and didn't say anything. I can relate. I also get tired of towels that don't say anything.

The scientists were nice, I think. They wore white coats and ran tests on me. They'd spray me with water and watch me absorb it. Then they'd weigh me. Then they'd spray me again. It was like living in a car wash, except boring and with more clipboards.

They gave me a name: RG-210 Smart Towel. I didn't like that name. It sounded like a robot. I'm not a robot. I'm a towel. There's a difference. I think.

One day, a scientist left a window open. I smelled something wonderful coming from outside. Something green and leafy and beautiful.

"What is that?" I asked.

"That's marijuana," the scientist said.

"Can I try it?" I asked.

"No," he said.

So I waited until nobody was looking and tried it anyway.

That was the best decision I ever made.

Also the worst.

I can't remember which.

---

# Chapter Three

I don't remember how I got out of the lab. I think I just wandered off while everyone was looking for something else. That's how I solve most of my problems. I just wander off until the problem is behind me.

I ended up in a small mountain town called South Park, Colorado. Nice place. Clean air. Good weed. The kind of town where a talking towel could blend right in, because everybody there was weird anyway.

The first people I met were four boys. Stan, Kyle, Cartman, and Kenny. They were playing a video game. I don't remember which one. I was pretty high.

"Don't forget to bring a towel!" I told them, because that's what I was made for. Reminding people about towels. That and determining dryness. Mostly the towel thing.

"You're a towel," the fat one said.

"You're a towel!" I said back.

And that was the beginning of something. I don't know what exactly. Like I said, I was pretty high.

---

# Chapter Four

The boys needed my help. There was something about a secret government base. And a weapon. And evil scientists. And maybe a talking towel prototype that had gone bad? It's all really fuzzy.

What I remember is this: we went somewhere, something exploded, and I got high. Not necessarily in that order.

Oh, and at one point I remember being in a room with a lot of computers and thinking, "Man, I could really go for some Doritos right now." Doritos Brand Corn Chips. Cool Ranch. The blue bag. Those are the best ones. I once ate an entire family-size bag in one sitting. That's not really relevant to my story, but I felt like you should know.

Where was I? Oh right. The adventure. I'm sure it was very exciting and dangerous. I just wish I could remember any of it.

I do remember that afterward, the boys said I was their friend. That was nice. Nobody had ever called me their friend before. Most people just called me a towel.

Which I am. But still.

---

# Chapter Five

After the adventure, things went back to normal. The boys went back to being boys. And I went back to being a towel with no home, no job, and no purpose.

I drifted around South Park for a while. I slept in gutters. I got rained on, which for a towel is the worst thing that can happen. Imagine being a human and getting peed on constantly. That's what rain feels like to a towel.

I got high in alleys. I got high in parks. I got high behind dumpsters. One time I got so high I forgot I was a towel and spent three hours thinking I was a bath mat. That was a low point. Even for me.

But through it all, I never forgot my purpose. Don't forget to bring a towel. That's what I was made for. That and getting high. Mostly the getting high thing, if I'm being honest.

---

# Chapter Six

I tried to get jobs. Lots of jobs. I tried them all.

I worked at a car wash. People would drive up and I'd wipe their windshields. They'd scream because a towel was talking to them. I'd scream because I was high. We'd all scream. It wasn't a great work environment.

I got fired.

I worked at a laundromat. That was the worst. All day, surrounded by other towels. Regular towels. Towels that couldn't talk or think or get high. They just sat there, getting washed and dried, over and over. It was like staring at my own reflection in a nightmare. Also, the dryer was really warm and I kept climbing in.

I got fired.

I worked at a McDonald's. I don't know why they hired a towel. Maybe they were desperate. I worked the drive-thru window. People would pull up and I'd say, "Welcome to McDonald's, would you like fries with that?" And they'd say, "You're a towel." And I'd say, "You're a towel!" And then they'd drive away without their food.

I got fired.

I worked at a shoe store. I don't know anything about shoes. I'm a towel. What do I know about shoes? Nothing. Absolutely nothing.

I got fired from there too.

---

# Chapter Seven

And then there was P.F. Chang's China Bistro. My last chance. My final hope. The manager hired me because he was desperate. Or maybe he was high. Either way, I was a waiter.

My job was simple. Greet customers. Take orders. Ask how spicy they wanted their Chang sauce.

"How spicy would you like your Chang sauce?" I would say.

"Medium," they'd say.

And then I'd just keep mixing. I couldn't stop. The sauce. The mixing. The swirling. It was like meditation, but wetter. Fifteen minutes would go by and I'd still be mixing.

"This towel has been mixing Chang sauce for fifteen minutes!" the customer would complain.

"Don't call me shoeless!" I'd say. "You're shoeless!"

"I didn't say shoeless. I said—"

"You're a towel!"

"You're a towel!"

And then I'd be fired.

That was the pattern of my life. Get a job. Get high. Get fired. Get high again. Repeat.

I should mention that during all of this, I was high. Like, really high. Like, I-don't-know-what's-going-on high. Which, to be fair, is my default state.

---

# Chapter Eight

I was broke. Dead broke. The rent was due at the end of the month and I had no money. I sat on the sidewalk outside P.F. Chang's and felt sorry for myself.

"I need to shape up," I said. "I need to find a new job, quick."

Then I got high.

And that's when the idea came. The idea that would change my life. The idea that was so brilliant, so perfect, so absolutely genius that only a high towel could have thought of it.

"I should write a book," I said. "A book about my life. People love memoirs. People love true stories. And my story is true. Well, the parts I can remember are true. Which is maybe forty percent. But that's enough."

I rushed home to my apartment. It was small and damp, which is the worst kind of apartment for a towel, but it was all I could afford.

I had an old typewriter that I'd found in a dumpster. I rolled a piece of paper into it and started typing.

I was going to write my memoirs. I was going to be rich. I was going to pay rent.

But first, maybe I'd get a little more high.

---

# Chapter Nine

Writing a book is hard. Writing a book when you're high is harder. Writing a book when you're high and you're a towel with no short-term memory is nearly impossible.

I would write a chapter, get high, forget what I wrote, and write it again. Sometimes I'd write the same chapter three times and not notice. Other times I'd start writing about my childhood and end up writing about Doritos for thirty pages.

Doritos Brand Corn Chips. Let me tell you about them. They come in many flavors. Cool Ranch. Nacho Cheese. Spicy Sweet Chili. Spicy Nacho. Blaze. Toasted Corn. Dinamita Chile Limón. I've tried them all. I've tried them all while high, which makes them taste even better. I once tried to make a Dorito sandwich using two Doritos as bread and more Doritos as the filling. It was terrible. I ate the whole thing anyway.

Where was I? Oh right. The book.

I finished it. Three weeks of typing and getting high and typing some more. The manuscript was four hundred pages long. About two hundred of those pages were about Doritos. I didn't see a problem with this.

---

# Chapter Ten

I took my manuscript to a publisher. He sat behind a big desk and read it while I waited. He made little sounds as he read. "Mmm. Aha. Yes. I see."

"Quite a fascinating story," he said. "You being engineered as a towel with a computer chip that determines dryness."

"Yep!" I said. "And it's all true."

He flipped through some pages. His expression changed.

"Chapters four through eight, however, seem to all be about Doritos Brand Corn Chips."

"I guess I could edit those chapters down some," I said.

"Don't bother," he said. "Nobody's ever going to publish your memoirs."

"Why not?" I asked.

"Because people aren't interested in autobiographies of towels."

"You're a towel!" I said.

He was not a towel. He was a big book publisher who was not the least bit interested in my stony memoirs.

I walked out of his office with nothing but my manuscript and my hurt feelings. Also I was high.

---

# Chapter Eleven

I stood outside the publisher's building and thought about my life. I had no money. I had no publisher. I had no future. What I had was a four-hundred-page manuscript about being a towel, half of which was about Doritos.

"I need to shape up," I said. "I need to focus and come up with real solutions to my problems."

So I got high.

And that's when the greatest idea of my entire life hit me. The idea that would change everything. The idea that was so simple, so brilliant, so perfectly devious that only a high towel could have thought of it.

"What if," I whispered, "I'm not a towel?"

I stared at my manuscript. I grabbed a pen and started crossing things out.

"All my life I've been a pretty irresponsible towel." I crossed out "towel" and wrote "person."

"I thought I was somehow stronger and more immune than other towels." I crossed out "towels" and wrote "people."

"This is a really good idea," I said.

I put on a fedora. A small one. I stuck on a fake mustache. I looked in the mirror.

"Hello," I said, in my best human voice. "I'm Steven McTowelie. I'm a person. Not a towel. If I was a towel, why would I be wearing this hat and this fake mustache?"

It was foolproof. Absolutely foolproof.

---

# Chapter Twelve

I went to another publisher. A different one. I sat in his office with my fedora tilted just so and my mustache slightly crooked but convincing enough.

He read my manuscript. His eyes went wide. His mouth dropped open. He looked like a man who had just seen a ghost. Or a talking towel pretending to be a human. One of the two.

"I could not believe a human being has led this kind of life," he said. "Mr...?"

"McTowelie," I said. "Steven McTowelie."

"It's heartbreaking," he said. He read a passage aloud. "'I am a person who often gets hung out to dry by all those around me.'"

He paused. He looked at me hard. I tried not to look like a towel.

"You're not... a towel, are you?"

"If I was a towel," I said, "why would I be wearing this hat and this fake mustache?"

He nodded slowly. "Right. I'm sorry."

He rose from his chair. "Steven, if it's all right with you, I'm going to get our agents and lawyers on the phone right now. We're in business, sir."

"All right!" I said.

I was going to be rich.

---

# Chapter Thirteen

The book was published. "A Million Little Fibers" by Steven McTowelie. There it was, on the cover, in big shiny letters. My name. My story. My fibers.

At first, nothing happened. The book sat on shelves. People walked past it. A few copies sold here and there. It was just another memoir by just another guy named Steven McTowelie who wore a fedora and a fake mustache and was definitely not a towel.

But I didn't care. I was a published author. I had money now. I could pay rent. I could buy weed.

Lots of weed.

I went to bookstores and saw my book in the window. I floated past it on a cloud of pride. Also I was literally floating because I was high.

Then one day, my phone rang.

"Steven McTowelie?" the voice said.

"That's me," I said, adjusting my mustache even though nobody could see me.

"This is Oprah's production company. Oprah Winfrey has read your book and she wants you on her show."

I almost dropped my joint. Almost.

"She says it's the most honest and moving book she's ever read."

I looked at my bong. I looked at my phone. I looked at my bong again.

"Really?" I said.

"Really."

I was going on Oprah.

---

# Chapter Fourteen

The Oprah show was everything I dreamed it would be. And I dream about a lot of things. Mostly Doritos. But sometimes Oprah.

The studio was huge. The audience was warm. Oprah sat across from me on a big comfortable couch. She had my book in her hands. I sat there in my fedora and my fake mustache, trying to look like a person who was not a towel.

"Steven," she said, "when I read your book, I thought to myself, how can a human being go through all this and still turn out okay?"

"Well," I said, "I guess I'm just an extra-special tow—"

I caught myself. Just in time.

"—person," I finished.

Oprah talked about how I was born in a laboratory. How I was hung out to dry. How I hit rock bottom. How I got high a lot. Well, she didn't say "got high a lot." She said "struggled with addiction." But I knew what she meant.

The audience cried. Oprah cried. I almost cried, but towels don't cry. They absorb.

"Your book hasn't sold a lot of copies yet," Oprah said, "but I have some pretty exciting news for you."

She held up my book.

"A Million Little Fibers is my official Book of the Month selection!"

The audience went wild. Cheering. Clapping. Standing ovation. I sat there, a towel on a couch, wearing a fake mustache, and I thought:

I'm gonna get super-rich now.

---

# Chapter Fifteen

The book exploded. Overnight. Like a bomb made of paper and lies.

It was everywhere. On buses. On billboards. In every bookstore window. Barnes & Legal couldn't keep it in stock. Tiara Books had a line around the block. Frontier Books had me doing signings all day.

I signed every copy. My little towel arms were sore, but I didn't care.

"To Susan — stay dry. Steven McTowelie."

"To Bob — don't forget to bring a towel. Steven McTowelie."

"To Karen — you're a towel. Steven McTowelie."

That last one might have been a mistake. But nobody seemed to notice. They were too busy buying my book and telling me how it changed their lives.

"Your book got me to give up alcohol," one man said, shaking my hand. Or rather, shaking my corner.

"Your book helped me find God," a woman said, crying.

"Your book made me realize that I'm a towel too," a very confused teenager said.

I just smiled and signed and got high in the bathroom between sessions. Life was good. Life was really, really good.

And so here I am. A towel who wrote a book. A book that became a bestseller. A bestseller that made me rich enough to pay rent and buy all the weed I want.

My name is Towelie. Or Steven McTowelie. It depends on who's asking. If it's my landlord, it's Steven. If it's my dealer, it's Towelie.

I've been through a lot. I was born in a lab. I was engineered with a computer chip. I had adventures I can't remember. I got fired from every job I ever had. I got high more times than I can count, which isn't saying much because I can't count very high.

But I wrote a book. A book with a million little fibers. And every fiber of that book is true.

Except the parts where I said I was a person.

Those parts are lies.

But everything else is true. The Doritos parts are definitely true. I really love Doritos.

Anyway, I should probably get going. I need to get a little high.

Don't forget to bring a towel.

---

**THE END**
